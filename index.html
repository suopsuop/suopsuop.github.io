Development of the World Wide Web

Bandwidth

Bandwidth is the measurement of the maximum capacity of a wired or wireless communications link. Typically described as a measurement of bits, kilobits, megabits, (etc) that can be transmitted in 1 second, it is used to send data.[1]

Dial-Up ADSL NBN
A method of transmitting data over a phone line, while preserving the ability to make phone calls at the same time. It has a theoretical maximum of 20 mbps download and 1 mbps upload, however it is generally far less.

For example, a 3.5 megabyte audio file would generally take around 11 seconds. 		At 56 kb/s, it is the slowest connection type to have been used, and could generally only be reliably used for text. It also took over the house's phone line, meaning only one could be used at once.

For example, a 3.5 megabyte audio file would take nearly 9 minutes. 		NBN replaces the old copper wiring with optical fibre cabling. It can transmit with speeds up to 1gbps, although most users will see a cap around the 100mbps download mark, and around 20-40 mbps upload. It can be used concurrently with a phone line.

For example, a 3.5 megabyte audio file would take less than a second.

Bandwidth is by far the largest changer in the Online Multimedia industry. Increase in bandwidth allows for great data transmission, leading to:

  • More Multimedia elements on websites.
  • Higher resolution/quality everything (Videos, Graphics, Audio, etc).
  • Faster loading times, for everything web related.
  • Ability to stream video and audio.

This allows people to design and create larger scale and more intricate multimedia, with less worry for things like file size or transmission time.

Cloud Storage

Cloud storage is a model of computer storage that stores data in logical pools, with the actual physical storage often spanning multiple servers, sometimes in multiple locations. The machines are highly virtualised. A large selling point for companies is that you pay only for what you use.[2]

Cloud storage made the Multimedia industry far more efficient, as nearly everything Multimedia related can be stored in the cloud. This means that collaborators do not have to do things in person, and can instead collaborate anywhere. It also means that many resources are able to be hosted through the cloud, instead of individual's devices, such as vector graphics for websites and videos. [3]

Personal Cloud Public Cloud Private Cloud
A personal cloud is a cloud server being run by the individual, either on one's own hardware, or through a VPS service. The individual is responsible for everything, but they have far more control. A public cloud is a cloud service being run by a cloud vendor, who is liable for creating and overseeing the pool of digital assets shared between subscribers of their network. The individual uses a middleman application, e.g. Google Drive or Dropbox to interact with their files and storage, and they let the company do the heavy lifting. Private clouds are cloud systems used by companies on a case by case basis, often with the company being able to specify certain requests, especially in handling data. This is often used for companies handling sensitive information, or whose data is mandated by certain governments. The servers are also known and specific, guaranteeing more physical security.

Cloud Services (Software Subscription)

Cloud services are infrastructure, software or platforms hosted by a third party, made available to users over the internet. This drastically reduces prerequisites for the user, generally only needing a computer and access to the internet to access the services.

On the company's end, hardware is generally abstracted and virtualised, letting many users access only portions of each computer's computing resources. There are multiple types of cloud service solutions.

Infrastructure-as-a-Service (IaaS) Platforms-as-a-Service (PaaS) Software-as-a-Service (SaaS) Function-as-a-Service (FaaS)
Provides the end user with computing, networking, and storage resources. Provides the end user with a platform that applications can run on, as well as the IT infrastructure required for it to run. Provides the end user with a cloud application. Provides developers a system to build, run, and manage app packages, without managing the infrastructure.

Cloud services, on the multimedia side of things, have moved more and more applications cloud based (e.g. text processing applications, graphic design applications, even audio and video creation and manipulation), allowing for a greater number of people to use, interact and create multimedia. It has also lessened the computational requirements for multimedia creators.[4]

Many desktop applications, too, integrate cloud services into their applications, e.g. Adobe, who has added AI processing into their applications but runs said models on their own hardware. This does come with the detriment of always needing an internet connection to use certain software.

It should be noted that at the current time most professional software solutions are not fully cloud based.

Streaming Services

Streaming services are a method of content delivery that streams data on command, without the user having to download it. This allows for large amounts of data to be received without needing high amounts of storage, e.g. 4K video. It also allows for easier copyright management, as it is harder for people to just access the file.[5]

It gives a more steady supply of money to the business, too, as streaming services generally operate on a pay-monthly basis.

Streaming services have revolutionised how most forms of video and audio are delivered, with platforms like Spotify and Apple Music dominating the music market, and platforms such as Netflix and Stan the film and television market. For multimedia, this allows for higher quality projects, as there is far less cost in physical distribution – the majority of people do not want high quality BluRays.

Social Media

Social media are interactive technologies that allow for and primarily facilitate the creation and sharing of information, ideas, messages, and other content like videos or audio. They allow for users of the internet to interact with each other in a variety of ways, and to build online audiences/followings.[6]

Social media has existed as soon as technology could support them, debuting as email and chat rooms in the early 70s. USENET revolutionised this by adding persistent networks in the form of discussion groups in 1979.[7]

Social media has given multimedia artists a platform to thrive on, allowing them to build a loyal fanbase and share their work. It has also created new job opportunities, especially around advertising and commission work.
